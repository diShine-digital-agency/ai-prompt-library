# Investor Pitch Deck Outline

Generate a complete investor pitch deck outline with slide-by-slide content
and speaker notes. Follows the structure investors expect — problem, solution,
market, traction, team, and ask — with guidance on what makes each slide
compelling.

## When to Use

- Preparing for a seed, Series A, or later fundraising round
- Pitching to angel investors or accelerator programs
- Creating a pitch deck for an internal venture or new business unit
- Coaching founders on pitch structure and storytelling

## The Technique

Provide your company details, the problem you solve, your solution, market
size data, business model, and any traction metrics. The model produces a
slide-by-slide outline with content guidance and speaker notes. Each slide
includes what to show, what to say, and the common mistakes that make
investors tune out.

The key insight: investors see thousands of decks. They pattern-match for
conviction, clarity, and evidence. Vague claims and feature lists lose
attention. Specific metrics and clear narrative hold it.

## Template

```
Generate an investor pitch deck outline for {{company_name}}.

## Problem
{{problem}}
Who has this problem and why existing solutions fail them.

## Solution
{{solution}}
What you built, how it works, and why it is meaningfully better.

## Market Size
{{market_size}}
TAM, SAM, SOM with sources. Include growth rate and market tailwinds.

## Business Model
{{business_model}}
Pricing, unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback period), revenue model.

## Traction
{{traction}}
Revenue (ARR/MRR), growth rate, customer count, retention, usage metrics.

## Output
Produce a slide-by-slide outline:
1. **Title** — company name, one-line description, round.
2. **Problem** — the pain point, who has it, cost of the status quo.
3. **Solution** — what you built and how it solves the problem.
4. **Why now** — what changed that makes this timely.
5. **Market size** — TAM → SAM → SOM with methodology.
6. **Product** — how it works in 3-4 steps.
7. **Business model** — pricing, unit economics, path to margin.
8. **Traction** — growth over time with inflection points highlighted.
9. **Competition** — 2x2 matrix showing your unique position.
10. **Team** — why this team wins.
11. **Financials** — 3-year projections with key assumptions.
12. **The ask** — amount, use of funds, milestones the capital unlocks.

For each slide provide: headline, 3-5 bullets of content, speaker notes,
and one common mistake to avoid.
```

## Examples

### Series A SaaS Pitch

```
Slide 3 — Solution
Headline: "[Product] automates compliance reporting in 10 minutes, not 10 days."

Content:
- Before: Compliance teams manually compile reports from 6+ systems,
  taking 40+ hours per audit cycle.
- After: One-click report generation pulling live data from all connected
  systems. Average time to audit-ready report: 12 minutes.
- Visual: Side-by-side before/after showing the old spreadsheet workflow
  vs. the product dashboard.

Speaker notes: "Walk through one concrete customer example. 'Acme Corp
went from 3 FTEs spending a week on SOC 2 prep to one person pressing
a button. They passed their audit with zero findings.' Then pause.
Let that land."

Common mistake: Showing a feature list instead of transformation.
Investors don't care about features — they care about the magnitude of
improvement.
```

## Tips

1. **Lead with a story** — Open with a specific customer, not a market stat.
   "Sarah spends 40 hours a month on X" beats "The X market is worth $5B."

2. **One takeaway per slide** — If a slide has two messages, split it. Make
   sure the headline alone tells the story.

3. **Show, don't claim** — "We have the best product" is a claim. "Customers
   switch from Competitor X in 3 days and see 40% improvement" is evidence.

4. **Rehearse the "why now" answer** — If your answer is "because we started
   the company," that is not a market insight. Point to a genuine inflection.

5. **Know your numbers cold** — Investors will interrupt with questions about
   CAC, LTV, and burn rate. Hesitating on unit economics signals you are
   not close enough to the business.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Starting with the solution** — Investors need to feel the problem
   before they care about the solution.

2. **Claiming "no competitors"** — Every company has competitors, even if
   they are spreadsheets or doing nothing. Saying you have none signals
   naivety or a nonexistent market.

3. **TAM from a research report with no bottoms-up math** — Build the
   number from unit economics: customers × price × frequency.

4. **Burying traction** — If you have strong metrics, move traction earlier.
   Nothing builds investor confidence faster than a growth chart.

5. **Vague use of funds** — "We will use the capital for growth" is not a
   plan. Break it down: "60% engineering, 25% sales, 15% ops." Show that
   each dollar maps to a milestone.
